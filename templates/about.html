What is the freedom in the world dataset?

The Freedom in the World dataset by Freedom House is an essential tool for evaluating the state of freedom across the globe. This dataset, spanning from 2013 through 2023, consistently uses the same indicators to provide a comprehensive analysis. For data-analysis purposes, Freedom House categorizes countries and territories into six distinct world regions: Africa, the Americas, Asia-Pacific, Eurasia, Europe, and the Middle East. It's important to highlight that, prior to the 2022 edition, countries in North Africa were grouped with the Middle East rather than with the rest of Africa. On this website we only use the 2023 edition, as comparing different years to each other requires a different approach. The approach that was chosen for on this website highlights a combination of this dataset with a different one. The dataset includes various metrics to measure freedom, such as the Political Rights Rating (PR Rating) and the Civil Liberties Rating (CL Rating). These ratings are further broken down into subcategories. For instance, the Electoral Process, Political Pluralism and Participation, and Functioning of Government subcategories provide detailed insights into the political rights landscape. Similarly, civil liberties are assessed through subcategories like Freedom of Expression and Belief, Associational and Organizational Rights, Rule of Law, and Personal Autonomy and Individual Rights. Additionally, the dataset includes scores for aggregate categories and specific discretionary questions.



What is the Surveillance Companies dataset?

Privacy International's Surveillance Industry Index (SII) is the most comprehensive publicly available database on the private surveillance sector. Compiled over four years, the SII includes 1,203 documents detailing 97 surveillance technologies from 338 companies across 36 countries. This research is part of the Big Brother Incorporated project, which investigates the sale of surveillance technologies by Western companies to repressive regimes. The findings highlight the alarming capabilities of these technologies, such as remote access to computers, mass interception of communications, social network monitoring, and location tracking of mobile phones. These technologies are often sold to regimes that use them to stifle dissent and target activists, posing significant human rights risks. The SII aims to expand public understanding of the surveillance industry by providing detailed analysis and contextual information. This transparency is crucial for advocating effective oversight and regulation of the industry. By offering comprehensive information on the private surveillance sector, the SII empowers the public to demand accountability and engage in informed discussions about the impact of these technologies on privacy and freedom of expression. The usage of this dataset on the website is not the best of its capabilities, as it is used to show the presence of surveillance companies in countries. This is done by counting the number of companies in a country and comparing it to the freedom of that country. As there are only 36 countries in the dataset, the results are not as accurate as they could be.
